Porcelain vs Ceramic Tiles for Importers and Project Buyers

How buyers should compare body, application, performance, handling and installation requirements.

Home›Blog›Porcelain vs Ceramic Tiles for Importers and Project Buyers
By LuxeCeram Export TeamPublished 2026-07-22

Porcelain and ceramic can look similar, but procurement must match the product body and performance to the actual application.

This guide is written for importers, distributors, showrooms and project buyers. It provides a procurement framework rather than a promise about stock, certification, customs rules, price or lead time. Confirm every technical and commercial detail for the exact product and destination.

What the buyer should evaluate

  • Define floor, wall, indoor, outdoor or wet-area use.
  • Review technical information relevant to the project.
  • Confirm glazed, unglazed, rectified, polished or matt description.
  • Check thickness, weight, packing and installation implications.
  • Use destination standards instead of generic marketing labels.

Ask for answers in writing and connect them to the quotation or product schedule. Critical requirements should identify who verifies them, when verification happens and what evidence is retained.

How to compare supplier offers

Compare suppliers only after product specification, quantity, packing, delivery term and exclusions have been normalised. A low unit price can be misleading when inspection, stronger packing, origin transport, freight or document support are outside the quotation.

Create a comparison sheet with mandatory requirements, acceptable alternatives and open clarifications. Request a revised quotation whenever the scope changes.

Documents and confirmations

  • Detailed proforma invoice and product schedule.
  • Packing list, pallet data and loading assumptions.
  • Technical data and test information applicable to the offered product.
  • Inspection, origin and transport documents required for the transaction.

Document names and legal requirements vary by destination. The importer should confirm current customs, conformity, tax and licensing requirements with qualified local professionals before shipment.

Risk controls

  • Using a wall product on a floor without technical approval.
  • Selecting a smooth finish where slip performance matters.
  • Assuming every porcelain product suits every exterior use.
  • Ignoring substrate and installation requirements.

Risk cannot be eliminated, but staged approvals, identity checks, traceable samples, defined inspection scope and a clear shipment document process reduce uncertainty.

Practical RFQ checklist

  • Buyer company and destination market.
  • Exact product, application and quantity by item.
  • Packing, pallet, labels and warehouse constraints.
  • Required samples, documents and inspection evidence.
  • Delivery term, target timing and acceptable alternatives.

Send the same structured RFQ to shortlisted suppliers and keep a clarification log. This is the simplest way to receive comparable quotations.
